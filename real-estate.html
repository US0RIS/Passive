Why Real Estate?

Real estate has created more millionaires than any other asset class. It offers multiple ways to build wealth: monthly cash flow from rent, appreciation over time, tax advantages, and the ability to use leverage (other people's money) to amplify returns.

Unlike stocks, real estate is a tangible asset you can see, touch, and improve. You have direct control over your investment's performance through property selection, management, and value-add improvements.

You don't need to be a landlord to invest in real estate. REITs, crowdfunding platforms, and syndications let you invest passively with as little as $10—earning rental income without dealing with tenants or toilets.

Real Estate Investment Options

1. REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts)

REITs are companies that own income-producing real estate and trade on stock exchanges. They're required to pay out 90% of taxable income as dividends, making them excellent for passive income.

  • Pros: Highly liquid, diversified, low minimums ($1+), no management required
  • Cons: Less control, stock market volatility, dividends taxed as ordinary income
  • Best for: Beginners, hands-off investors, those wanting liquidity

2. Real Estate Crowdfunding

Platforms like Fundrise, RealtyMogul, and CrowdStreet let you invest in commercial properties or portfolios with minimums of $10-$25,000. You earn returns from both cash flow and appreciation.

  • Pros: Access to commercial deals, diversification, professional management
  • Cons: Illiquid (5-7 year holds), platform risk, fees
  • Best for: Accredited investors seeking passive commercial exposure

3. Rental Properties

Owning physical rental properties provides the highest returns and control but requires active involvement or hiring property management (typically 8-10% of rent).

  • Pros: Full control, leverage, tax benefits, forced appreciation through improvements
  • Cons: High capital requirements, management hassles, concentration risk
  • Best for: Hands-on investors with $50K+ and willingness to learn

4. House Hacking

Buy a multi-unit property, live in one unit, and rent the others. Your tenants pay your mortgage while you build equity. The ultimate way to get started with minimal capital.

  • Pros: Low down payment (3.5% FHA), learn landlording with training wheels
  • Cons: Living near tenants, property limitations
  • Best for: First-time buyers willing to sacrifice some privacy for wealth building

Getting Started with REITs (Easiest Path)

1

Open a Brokerage Account

Use any major broker (Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard) or app (M1 Finance, Robinhood). Ensure it supports DRIP for automatic dividend reinvestment.

2

Choose Your REIT Strategy

Start with diversified REIT ETFs like VNQ (Vanguard Real Estate) or SCHH (Schwab US REIT). For higher yields, consider individual REITs like O (Realty Income) or STAG (industrial properties).

3

Consider Tax-Advantaged Accounts

REIT dividends are taxed as ordinary income. Holding REITs in a Roth IRA eliminates taxes on dividends entirely—maximizing your effective returns.

4

Invest Consistently

Set up automatic monthly investments. REITs can be volatile short-term but have delivered 10%+ annual returns historically. Time in market beats timing the market.

Key Metrics for Rental Properties

Cash-on-Cash Return

Annual cash flow divided by total cash invested. Aim for 8-12% minimum.

Cap Rate

Net operating income divided by property price. Indicates return if you paid all cash. 5-10% is typical depending on market.

The 1% Rule

Monthly rent should be at least 1% of purchase price. A $200K property should rent for $2,000/month minimum.

50% Rule

Expect 50% of gross rent to go toward expenses (not including mortgage). Use this for quick analysis before diving deeper.
